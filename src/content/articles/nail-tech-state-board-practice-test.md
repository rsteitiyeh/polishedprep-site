A nail technician state board exam practice test drills the four areas your written licensing exam covers: sanitation and infection control, nail anatomy and disorders, manicure and pedicure procedure, and enhancements (acrylics, gels, wraps). Here are 6 sample questions we wrote, with the answer key explained, plus the weighting to study by.

Most states test nail techs (manicurists) with a shorter written exam than the full cosmetology test, often built on the NIC nail technology outline and delivered at PSI centers; both are named here descriptively only. Shorter doesn't mean easier per question. With fewer total items, each miss costs more, and sanitation questions are thick on the ground.

6 sample nail tech practice questions

Answer all six before checking the key. Real exams commonly run 60 to 80 questions in 90 minutes, roughly a minute each, so treat these the same way (California's manicurist exam is 60 scored plus 5 pretest questions in 90 minutes).

1.  **Q1.** A foot spa basin must be cleaned and disinfected: (A) at the end of each day (B) after every client, with the disinfectant circulating for the label's full contact time (C) weekly with bleach (D) only when visibly dirty

2.  **Q2.** A client shows greenish discoloration under a lifting acrylic nail. The technician should: (A) buff it out and refill (B) apply antifungal polish (C) remove the enhancement if appropriate, refuse further enhancement work, and refer to a physician (D) soak the nail in acetone and continue

3.  **Q3.** The half-moon shaped, whitish area at the base of the nail plate is the: (A) lunula (B) hyponychium (C) eponychium (D) nail mantle

4.  **Q4.** Onycholysis refers to: (A) ingrown nails (B) separation of the nail plate from the nail bed (C) thickened nails (D) bitten nails

5.  **Q5.** During acrylic application, the correct mix ratio of liquid monomer to powder polymer forms: (A) a runny bead that spreads flat (B) a dry, crumbly bead (C) a medium-wet bead that holds its shape briefly before self-leveling (D) any consistency, since ratio doesn't matter

6.  **Q6.** Metal implements like cuticle nippers must be, in order: (A) disinfected, then cleaned (B) cleaned of debris, then fully immersed in an EPA-registered disinfectant for the labeled contact time (C) wiped with alcohol between clients (D) sterilized in UV light only

Answer key with explanations

| 1     | B          | Foot spas are the highest-risk equipment in a nail salon; boards require clean-and-disinfect after every client, plus documented periodic deep cleaning in many states.                          |
| 2     | C          | Green discoloration suggests a pseudomonas bacterial infection. Diagnosis and treatment are outside a nail tech's scope; enhancements don't go over infected nails.                              |
| 3     | A          | The lunula is the visible front of the matrix. The eponychium is the living skin fold at the base; the hyponychium sits under the free edge.                                                     |
| 4     | B          | Onycholysis is plate-from-bed separation, often from trauma or product misuse. It means no enhancement work over the affected area.                                                              |

The disorder table worth memorizing

Since disorders decide more borderline scores than any other topic, here's the study move that works: attach an action to every term, because that's how the exam frames them. Onychomycosis (fungal infection): refuse and refer. Paronychia (bacterial infection of the fold): refuse and refer. Onycholysis: no product over the separated area. Beau's lines, ridges, bruised nails without broken skin: generally serviceable with judgment. Bitten nails: serviceable, and enhancements are often appropriate. Green pseudomonas discoloration under an enhancement: remove, refuse, refer. Drill the term-to-action pairs rather than the definitions alone, and those questions become the fastest points on the test.

The reasoning behind the pattern is scope of practice. Nail techs beautify healthy nails; anything infected, inflamed, or diagnosable belongs to a physician. When two answers both seem defensible, the more conservative one wins, and "refer to a physician" is nearly always among the credited responses when infection is on the table.

Chemistry questions: the second-fastest points

Enhancement chemistry sounds intimidating and tests simply. Know that monomer liquid and polymer powder cure through a chain reaction started by an initiator; that UV and LED gels cure through photoinitiators, which is why uncured gel against skin causes overexposure risk; that primers improve adhesion and shouldn't touch soft tissue; and that acetone removes acrylic while gel often requires filing plus soaking. Add the mix-ratio fact from Q5 (medium bead, holds then self-levels) and the "why" behind lifting (oil, moisture, or improper prep at the nail plate) and you've covered the enhancement questions that appear on nearly every form of the exam.

Notice what all of these have in common: they're explanations, not trivia. When you can say why a too-wet bead weakens the enhancement, the four answer options sort themselves.

Pacing a shorter exam

With roughly 100 questions and a pass line around 70 to 75 percent, you can miss about 25 questions and still pass, which should lower your shoulders a little. The pacing plan: first pass through the whole exam answering everything, flagging only genuine guesses; second pass on flagged items only; stop. On a 90-minute clock, that first pass should take about 65 minutes, leaving real review time. Candidates who stall two minutes on question 12 borrow that time from question 90, and the questions at the end are statistically no harder than the ones at the start.

FAQ

How many questions are on the nail tech state board exam?

Typically 60 to 80 multiple-choice questions with 90 minutes allowed, though a few states run longer forms (California's manicurist exam is 60 scored plus 5 pretest questions in 90 minutes). Your state board or its testing vendor publishes a candidate bulletin with the exact count, time limit, and cut score; read it before you schedule.

What is the hardest part of the nail tech written exam?

Nail diseases and disorders, by reader consensus. The terms look alike (onychosis, onycholysis, onychomycosis) and the exam tests whether you can match each condition to the right action: proceed, modify the service, or refuse and refer. Flashcards with the refer/don't-refer decision attached to each term work well.

Do nail techs take a practical exam too?

Many states require one, either live or via school certification, covering setup, sanitation, manicure procedure, and enhancement application on a model hand. Kit and labeling rules mirror the cosmetology practical, so pack from your state's official list and label every container.

How long should I study for the nail technician exam?

Two to three weeks of 8 to 10 focused hours weekly is enough for most recent graduates. Start with a full diagnostic test, spend the middle weeks on your weak topics, and finish with alternating-day full-length tests until scores hold above 80 percent.
