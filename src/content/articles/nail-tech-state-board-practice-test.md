A nail technician state board practice test should mirror the National-Interstate Council of State Boards of Cosmetology's Nail Technology Theory Examination, split into Scientific Concepts, worth 40 percent of the exam, and Nail Technology Procedures, worth 60 percent. The exam runs 110 total items, 100 scored, over 90 minutes, and exam fees for the theory and practical portions typically run in the range of $84 to $150 combined depending on the state and testing vendor.

Below, we break down what's actually in each domain and six original sample questions written in the same style most state exams use.

## What's on the Scientific Concepts domain (40%)?

This covers infection control principles (the differences between sanitation, disinfection, and sterilization), cross-contamination prevention, blood exposure procedures, basic anatomy and physiology as it relates to the hands, feet, and nails, and basic chemistry of nail products including how different chemical categories interact with the natural nail plate. It's the smaller of the two domains, but it's foundational to nearly every procedural question in the larger section, since procedure questions frequently layer a safety or sanitation twist on top of a technique question.

## What's on the Nail Technology Procedures domain (60%)?

This is the larger domain and covers client consultation and documentation, tools used in nail services (nippers, files, buffers, pedicure basins), basic manicure and pedicure procedure from start to finish, and the application, maintenance, and removal of nail enhancements including tips and acrylics. Because this domain is worth more than half the exam, procedural sequence questions, meaning "what comes next" or "what should have happened before this step," show up more often here than pure definition questions.

## 6 sample nail technician practice questions (with answers explained)

1. **What is the correct order of steps for disinfecting a multi-use nail implement between clients?**
Clean to remove debris and product residue first, then fully immerse the implement in an EPA-registered disinfectant for the full contact time listed on the product label. Skipping the cleaning step lets residue shield contaminants from the disinfectant, which defeats the purpose of the soak entirely.

2. **A client's toenail shows signs of a fungal infection before a pedicure. What's the correct response?**
Refuse the pedicure service on the affected nail and refer the client to a physician. Performing a service over a suspected infection is a contraindication violation regardless of what the client requests, and it's one of the most consistently tested safety scenarios on nail exams.

3. **What is the purpose of a primer in acrylic nail enhancement application?**
It improves adhesion between the natural nail plate and the acrylic product by adjusting the nail surface's pH and removing surface oils. Skipping or misapplying primer is a common cause of lifting.

4. **During a basic manicure, what's the correct sequence for cuticle care?**
Soften the cuticle first (typically with a cuticle remover or soak), then gently push it back, and only trim visibly loose or hanging tissue rather than cutting into live cuticle tissue. Aggressive cutting into attached cuticle is scored as a technique and safety error on most state practicals.

5. **What does "virgin application" mean when applying nail enhancements?**
Applying product to a nail that has never had enhancement product on it before, as opposed to a "fill" or "rebalance," which only addresses new growth on an already-enhanced nail. Exams frequently test whether a candidate can distinguish a virgin application from a fill procedure.

6. **A client requests a pedicure, and you notice a cut or break in the skin on their foot before starting. What should you do?**
Avoid the affected area, apply standard blood exposure precautions if there's any bleeding, and adjust or decline the service around that specific spot rather than proceeding as normal. Broken skin is treated as a contraindication zone, not something to work around cosmetically.

## How should you split your study time between the two domains?

Since Nail Technology Procedures outweighs Scientific Concepts by a fair margin, 60 percent versus 40 percent, most of your remaining prep time should go toward procedural sequences: what step comes before another, which tool serves which stage, and where infection control requirements intersect with a specific service. Scientific Concepts still deserves real study time, since procedural questions often lean on a safety or sanitation detail buried inside them, but it shouldn't get equal hours with the larger domain just because it feels more like "the science part."

## FAQ

**How many questions are on the nail technician state board exam?**
110 total items in the NIC national Nail Technology Theory Examination, of which 100 are scored and 10 are unscored pretest questions.

**What's the passing score for the nail technician exam?**
Most states set it in the 70 to 75 percent range on the scored items, though the exact cut score comes from your individual state board, not from NIC itself.

**Which domain is worth more, science or procedures?**
Nail Technology Procedures, at 60 percent of the exam versus 40 percent for Scientific Concepts, based on NIC's own content outline for the national nail technology exam.

**How much does the nail technician exam cost?**
Combined theory and practical exam fees typically run $84 to $150 depending on the state and testing vendor, on top of any license application fee your state charges separately.

**Is the nail technician exam the same everywhere?**
Most states use the NIC national theory exam as their base, though some states administer their own separate exam. Check your state board's candidate information bulletin to confirm which version you'll take.
