A cosmetology state board kit is the bag of supplies you bring to the practical exam: a mannequin head, cutting and styling tools, chemical service simulation products, and sanitation supplies, all labeled and packed to your state's published list. Prebuilt kits run about $150 to $350; assembling your own from school supplies usually costs less. The state's candidate bulletin is the only official list.

Kit requirements vary by state, and evaluators can refuse items that violate labeling or safety rules, which is how prepared candidates end up rattled before the first timed section starts. The list below reflects the common core across NIC-style practical exams (NIC named descriptively; we're not affiliated). Cross-check every line against your own state's bulletin.

The single most common kit failure we hear about isn't a missing tool. It's labeling. "All products in original or labeled containers" appears in nearly every state bulletin, and a spray bottle of mystery liquid gets pulled no matter what's actually in it. Label everything, even the water.

Self-assembling from beauty-supply retail runs roughly $270 at 2026 prices using mid-grade tools, and much less if your school kit already covers shears and a dryer, which it usually does. Prebuilt "state board kits" from supply houses cost $150 to $350 and save shopping time, but check their contents line by line against your state list anyway; we've seen prebuilt kits missing labeled disinfectant containers, which is the one category evaluators check first.

Our advice after watching readers do both: build from your school kit, buy the gaps, and spend the savings on a second mannequin head for practice runs. Rehearsal value beats packaging.

Packing and exam-day logistics

1.  Pack from your state's official list, not this or any other website's version. Print the list and check items off physically.

2.  Label every container with its contents, including transferred products and water bottles.

3.  Bag single-use items in sealed packs so evaluators can see they're new.

4.  Do a full dress rehearsal: unpack, set up your station, run each timed section, repack. Twice. The 4-week schedule at PassCosmo/cosmetology-state-board-study-guide slots these rehearsals into weeks 3 and 4.

5.  The night before, stage the bag by the door with your ID and admission documents inside it, not next to it.

The mannequin question, answered properly

The mannequin head causes more pre-exam anxiety than any other kit item, and the rules deserve their own section. Most states require human-hair or majority-human-hair mannequins for sections involving chemical simulation or thermal work, because synthetic fiber behaves differently under heat and water. Hair length minimums (commonly around 6 inches in the crown, where specified) exist so you can actually demonstrate the required cuts and wraps. And the head must be new enough to perform every tested section: a head you've already cut short for practice can't demonstrate a long-layer section. Budget for two heads, one to rehearse on and one that arrives at the exam ready. At $25 to $50 per decent human-hair head, the second one is the cheapest insurance in your whole kit.

Check your bulletin for clamp rules too. Some sites specify table clamps only, and a candidate with a tripod-style stand and no clamp starts the timed setup section improvising.

What evaluators actually watch during setup

The setup period isn't dead time before the real exam; in most states it's a scored section. Evaluators watch whether your supplies come out organized, whether dirty and clean items get separate zones, whether your disinfectant is labeled and your wet sanitizer is set up before any tool touches the mannequin, and whether you sanitize your hands before beginning. The candidates who struggle here aren't missing items, they're fumbling for them, which is why the dress rehearsals matter: unpacking your own bag should be muscle memory. Lay the kit out the same way every practice run, left to right in order of use, and exam-day setup becomes the calmest five minutes of the test.

A small real-world detail readers keep confirming: bring more towels than the list requires. Towels are the item most often dropped, contaminated, or used up mid-section, and a dropped towel is unusable the moment it hits the floor. Extras cost nothing and rescue sections.

Kit rules for estheticians and nail techs

The labeling and single-use principles are identical across license types, but the contents shift. Esthetician kits center on disposables: spatulas, cotton, applicators, sealed simulated products, plus headbands and linens. Nail kits add an important wrinkle: implements like nippers and pushers must arrive cleaned and disinfected with proof of proper storage, and abrasive files must be visibly new. If you're testing for one of those licenses, the same bulletin-first rule applies, and the theory side of your prep lives at PassCosmo/nail-tech-state-board-practice-test for nail candidates.

FAQ

What is in a cosmetology state board kit?

A mannequin head and clamp, haircutting tools, perm and color simulation supplies, thermal styling tools, labeled sanitation products, towels and capes, and (where tested) disposable skin and nail implements, all packed to the state's published kit list. The candidate bulletin from your state board defines the exact required contents.

How much does a state board kit cost?

Prebuilt kits run about $150 to $350 from beauty supply houses. Self-assembly costs roughly $270 at 2026 retail if you're starting from nothing, and far less when your school kit covers the big-ticket tools. Either way, verify contents against your state's list; missing items cost more than any kit.

Can I bring real chemicals to the practical exam?

Usually no. Most states require simulated products (conditioner standing in for relaxer or waving lotion, for example) in labeled containers, precisely so no live chemistry happens on site. A few jurisdictions differ, which is why the bulletin's product rules are worth reading twice.

Will the exam site provide anything?

Assume nothing beyond a table and chair. Some sites offer outlets for dryers; others score thermal sections as dry demonstrations. The bulletin states what's provided, and packing as if the answer is "nothing" has never hurt a candidate.

How early should I have my kit ready?

Two weeks before exam day, fully packed, so both dress rehearsals happen with the real bag. That timeline leaves room to replace anything that breaks or turns out to be non-compliant, and shipping delays on a mannequin head are a solved problem in week minus-two and a crisis in week zero.

Bottom line

Print your state's official kit list, build from your school kit, label every container, and rehearse the full setup twice before exam day. The kit itself is a $150 to $350 problem at most; showing up with an unlabeled bottle is the failure that actually stings.
