An esthetician practice test simulates your state's written licensing exam: multiple-choice questions on sanitation, skin histology, facial treatments, hair removal, and makeup. Below are 8 sample questions we wrote with full answer explanations, plus the section weighting most exams follow and a study loop that turns practice scores into a license.

Most states build their esthetician written exam from the NIC national outline or a state-specific version of it, usually delivered through PSI testing centers. 8 sample esthetician practice exam questions

We wrote these to match state board phrasing: one clearly right answer, one tempting near-miss, two throwaways. Answer all 8 before checking the key. Most states give you roughly a minute per question (a common format is 75 to 100 questions in 90 minutes (California's esthetician exam is 75 scored plus 10 pretest questions in 90 minutes); your candidate bulletin has the exact numbers), so time yourself.

1.  **Q1.** Before and after every client service, an esthetician's hands must be: (A) wiped with a dry towel (B) washed with soap and warm water (C) sprayed with alcohol only (D) covered with the same gloves used previously

2.  **Q2.** The layer of the epidermis where new cells are produced through mitosis is the: (A) stratum corneum (B) stratum granulosum (C) stratum germinativum (basale) (D) stratum lucidum

3.  **Q3.** A client presents with widespread inflamed pustules and states they are under treatment for cystic acne. The esthetician should: (A) perform extractions carefully (B) apply a strong exfoliant first (C) refer the client to their physician and avoid working over active lesions (D) proceed with a standard facial

4.  **Q4.** Comedones with open, darkened surfaces are called: (A) milia (B) blackheads (open comedones) (C) papules (D) whiteheads

5.  **Q5.** During soft wax hair removal, the wax should be removed: (A) in the direction of hair growth, slowly (B) against the direction of hair growth, quickly and parallel to the skin (C) straight upward (D) in the direction of hair growth, quickly

6.  **Q6.** The Fitzpatrick scale classifies: (A) acne severity (B) skin's response to UV exposure (C) skin hydration levels (D) hair texture

7.  **Q7.** High-frequency current used in facial treatments produces a germicidal effect through: (A) galvanic desincrustation (B) ozone generated at the electrode (C) direct heat conduction (D) iontophoresis

8.  **Q8.** Disinfected, reusable implements must be stored: (A) in the technician's pocket (B) in a closed, clean, labeled container (C) on an open counter (D) in the original disinfectant overnight

Answer key with explanations

|       |            |                                                                                                                                                   |
| ----- | ---------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Q** | **Answer** | **Why it's right**                                                                                                                                |
| 1     | B          | Hand washing with soap and water is the baseline in every state's rules. Sanitizer supplements it; it never replaces it between clients.          |
| 2     | C          | The basale (germinativum) layer sits deepest and divides constantly. Corneum is the dead surface layer you exfoliate.                             |
| 3     | C          | Active cystic acne under medical care is a refer-out. Working over inflamed lesions risks spreading infection and exceeds an esthetician's scope. |
| 4     | B          | Open comedones oxidize and darken. Milia are closed keratin cysts; papules have no visible opening.                                               |
| 5     | B          | Soft wax strips come off fast, against growth, pulled parallel to the skin to remove the hair at the root without lifting skin.                   |
| 6     | B          | Fitzpatrick types I through VI describe how skin responds to UV. It drives choices in peels, laser referrals, and sun-damage education.           |
| 7     | B          | High-frequency (Tesla) current creates ozone at the glass electrode, which has a mild germicidal effect on the skin's surface.                    |
| 8     | B          | Rules in nearly every state require clean, covered, labeled storage so disinfected tools stay disinfected until use.                              |

The study loop that actually raises scores

1.  Take a full-length practice exam cold and record your score by domain.

2.  Study only your weakest domain for 3 to 4 days. Use your textbook (Milady's esthetics text is the common one in US programs) and rewrite key facts in your own words.

3.  Retest that domain with fresh questions. New questions matter; repeating the same set tests memory, not readiness.

4.  Repeat weekly until every domain clears 80 percent, then schedule the real exam.

The 80 percent target isn't arbitrary. Written pass marks in most states sit at 70 to 75 percent (your state bulletin lists the exact cut score), and practicing 5 to 10 points above the line absorbs exam-day nerves. How esthetician questions are built, and how to beat them

Go back to Q3, the cystic acne scenario. One answer is right, one is tempting ("perform extractions carefully" sounds diligent), and two are eliminable on sight, because nobody applies a strong exfoliant to inflamed skin and nobody proceeds with a standard facial over active cystic lesions. Nearly every board question follows this architecture, and it means partial knowledge still wins coins flips if you eliminate first and choose second.

Two reading habits pay for themselves. First, hunt for scope-of-practice framing: whenever a scenario involves a medical condition, medication, or anything that looks diagnosable, the answer that refers the client to a physician is right far more often than not, because estheticians work on healthy skin and the exam checks that you know your lane. Second, watch for absolute words. Options containing "always," "never," or "only" are wrong more often than right, while carefully hedged options survive. Exam writers hedge correct answers for legal accuracy; they write distractors bold.

Practice these habits under a timer from day one. Pacing is trained, not innate, and an untimed month of practice leaves you meeting time pressure for the first time on the day it costs money.

What to memorize cold versus what to understand

Some exam content is pure recall, and flashcards handle it: the layers of the epidermis in order, contact time rules, the Fitzpatrick types, which conditions are contagious (contraindicated) versus merely cosmetic. Other content is conceptual and flashcards fail: why desincrustation uses negative galvanic current while iontophoresis of acid products uses positive, or why soft wax removes against growth while hard wax can go either way. For the conceptual half, the test question is always some variation of "what happens if," so study by asking yourself the same thing. When you can explain the why to a classmate without notes, you own it.

A practical split that's worked for our readers: 20 minutes of flashcard recall daily (mornings, coffee), plus two or three deeper study blocks weekly for the conceptual material, plus one full timed practice exam on the weekend. That rhythm covers both kinds of knowledge without turning prep into a second job.

Exam day and what happens after

Expect computer-based testing with check-in, ID verification, and your phone in a locker. Most centers show your result before you leave. A failed attempt is recoverable everywhere: you rebook after a waiting period and pay a retake fee, commonly in the $50 to $110 range depending on state and vendor. What you should actually do with a fail is mine the score report, because it breaks out your performance by domain, and that breakdown is a free diagnostic pointing at exactly which chapter earns your next two weeks.

FAQ

How many questions are on the esthetician state board exam?

Commonly 75 to 100 multiple-choice questions with 90 minutes allowed, though states vary (California's esthetician exam is 75 scored plus 10 pretest questions in 90 minutes). Some exams include unscored experimental questions mixed in. The candidate information bulletin from your state board or its testing vendor lists the exact count, time limit, and passing score.

Is the esthetician exam hard?

First-time pass rates vary by state and school, but candidates who complete their program hours and take at least three full-length practice exams pass at noticeably higher rates in our reader surveys. The exam rewards steady review over cramming because so much of it is straightforward recall of sanitation rules and skin anatomy.

What's the difference between an esthetician practice exam and the NIC exam?

The NIC develops the national examination content many states license; a practice exam imitates its topics and difficulty using original questions. Real NIC items are confidential. A good practice test trains the same skill set: reading carefully, eliminating wrong answers, and pacing yourself to a minute per question.

Can I take the esthetician written exam before finishing school?

Some states allow testing near the end of your program hours; others require full completion and school certification first. Check your state board's application page before scheduling, because a rejected application can cost you a resubmission fee and several weeks.
