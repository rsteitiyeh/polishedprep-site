Cosmetology and barbering license renewal follows the same basic pattern in every state: renew on a fixed cycle (usually every 1 to 2 years), complete any required continuing education, and pay a fee, typically $25 to $100. Miss the deadline and a late penalty applies; lapse long enough and you may face reinstatement or retesting. The details, though, are set state by state.

There's no national license and no national renewal rule. The state board that issued your license owns your renewal, which is why the only authoritative source for your situation is that board's website. What we can give you is the pattern: what's typical, what varies, and which traps catch the most licensees.

The three variables that define your renewal

1\. Cycle length

Two-year cycles are the most common arrangement for both cosmetology and barbering licenses. Louisiana renews annually. New York runs four-year terms. Some states stagger expiration by birth date, others by issue date, and a few expire everyone on the same calendar day statewide, like Georgia's September 30 expiration for its cosmetology licenses (per sos.ga.gov).

2\. Continuing education

CE is where states differ most. Texas requires 4 hours of approved CE per two-year cycle for cosmetologists, dropping to 1 sanitation hour after 15 years licensed (tdlr.texas.gov). Louisiana requires none at all. Georgia tracks CE through the CE Broker system. When CE exists, sanitation or health-and-safety content is almost always the required core, and boards approve providers, so a random YouTube course doesn't count.

3\. Fees and penalties

On-time renewal fees mostly land between $25 and $100 per cycle. Late renewal usually adds a penalty of 50 to 100 percent of the fee, and most states define a hard cutoff (commonly 1 to 5 years lapsed) after which you stop being a "late renewal" and become a reinstatement case, sometimes with reexamination attached.

The five mistakes that cost licensees real money

1.  Renewing before CE hours post. Boards that require CE check electronically; if your provider hasn't reported, the renewal stalls and some systems make you start over.

2.  Assuming your barber and cosmetology licenses renew together. In most states they're separate credentials with separate dates and separate fees, even when one agency manages both.

3.  Letting a booth-rental or salon license lapse while renewing the personal one. Shop registrations have their own cycle.

4.  Missing mail because the board has an old address. Update your address with the board when you move; "I never got the notice" waives nothing.

5.  Working through the late window. Late renewal keeps your license recoverable, but practicing on an expired license is a citable violation in essentially every state, with fines that dwarf the renewal fee.

How to find your state's real requirements in five minutes

1.  Search for your state plus "cosmetology board" or "barber board" and go only to the .gov result. Ad-supported lookalike sites dominate these searches and are wrong often enough to matter.

2.  Find the license lookup or verification tool and pull your own record. Your actual expiration date beats any general rule on the site.

3.  Read the renewal page for three numbers: the fee, the CE hours, and the late-window cutoff. Screenshot it; boards reorganize their sites constantly.

4.  If CE is required, find the approved-provider list before buying a course. Unapproved hours are the most expensive kind of free time.

5.  Put two dates in your calendar: 60 days before expiration (start CE) and 14 days before (renew).

Moving states: what renewal history has to do with reciprocity

When you apply for a license in a new state, the application asks whether your existing license is current and whether you've ever been disciplined. A cleanly renewed license transfers through endorsement or reciprocity in most states with paperwork and a fee; a lapsed one complicates everything, sometimes forcing you through the new state's full examination. If a move is even on your horizon, keeping the old license current through the transition is cheap insurance. Several readers have renewed a license in a state they'd already left purely to keep the reciprocity path clean, and every one of them called it money well spent.

CE is the other moving-day surprise. Coming from a no-CE state like Louisiana into a CE state means your first renewal in the new state carries hour requirements you've never had to track. Set up whatever tracking system the new board uses (Georgia, for instance, runs completions through CE Broker per sos.ga.gov) the week you're licensed, not the week you renew.

Instructors, shops, and the licenses people forget

Individual operator licenses get all the attention, but three other credential types lapse quietly and cause outsized pain. Instructor licenses often carry their own CE requirements, frequently including teaching-methodology hours beyond the standard sanitation content. Salon and shop registrations renew on their own cycle, and an expired shop license exposes every licensee working inside it during inspections. Booth-rental permits, where states require them, are the most commonly forgotten of all because they feel like paperwork rather than licensure. If you hold more than one credential, list them all with expiration dates in one place today; the five minutes costs less than any single late penalty.

FAQ

How often do cosmetology and barbering licenses need to be renewed?

Most states use a two-year cycle for both. Louisiana renews annually and New York uses four-year terms, so check your own board. Your expiration date is printed on the license and visible in your state's online license lookup, which is the fastest way to confirm.

Do all states require continuing education for renewal?

No. Requirements range from zero (Louisiana) to several hours per cycle, and sanitation content is the common core where CE exists. Texas requires 4 hours per two-year cycle per tdlr.texas.gov. Always use board-approved providers, because unapproved hours simply don't count.

Can I renew a license that expired two years ago?

Often yes, with penalties, because many states allow late renewal for one to five years after expiration. Past your state's cutoff, you're into reinstatement, which can involve a new application and sometimes reexamination. Your board's website spells out the exact cutoff; find it before assuming either way.

Are barber and cosmetology renewals different?

The mechanics are nearly identical: cycle, CE if required, fee. The differences are administrative: some states run separate barber boards with their own fees and CE rules, while consolidated agencies like Texas TDLR manage both under aligned requirements. Dual license holders should confirm whether each credential renews separately.

Do renewal fees ever change?

Regularly. Boards adjust fees through legislation or rulemaking; Louisiana, for example, raised fees under a 2024 law announced at lsbc.louisiana.gov. Any fee figure more than a year old, including figures on this page, deserves a quick check against the board's current schedule. The checkout screen on your state's own renewal portal is always the number that counts.

Bottom line

Find your expiration date today, put a reminder 60 days ahead of it, and bookmark your state board's renewal page, because that page, not any blog including this one, is the final word on your fees and CE. Renewal is a $25 to $100 errand on time and a genuine problem late.
